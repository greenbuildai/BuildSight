1. Project Overview 
BuildSight is an integrated construction safety monitoring system that combines advanced 
computer vision technology with Geographic Information Systems (GIS) for comprehensive 
spatial risk analysis. The project addresses critical safety challenges in multi-storey construction 
sites through real-time worker detection, Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) compliance 
monitoring, and spatial risk visualization. 
1.1 Project Architecture 
The project follows a three-phase parallel execution strategy, ensuring simultaneous 
advancement of AI model development (Phase A) and GIS-based spatial analysis (Phase B) 
across all academic review cycles: 
•        Phase A: AI Vision Model Development & Evaluation 
•        Phase B: GIS-Based Spatial Safety Mapping 
This dual-track approach ensures that technical model development and civil engineering 
spatial relevance evolve synergistically, resulting in a cohesive safety monitoring solution. 
1.2 Research Objectives 
The primary objectives of this research are: 
1.     Develop and evaluate AI-powered computer vision models for real-time construction 
worker and PPE detection under diverse site conditions 
2.     Integrate spatial analysis capabilities through GIS to map worker density and identify 
high-risk zones across multi-storey construction sites 
3.     Address occlusion challenges inherent in crowded and multi-storey construction 
environments 
4.     Create a unified real-time safety monitoring application suitable for industry 
deployment 
 
 
1.3 Expected Contributions 
This research is expected to contribute to construction safety management through: 
•        Comparative analysis of state-of-the-art computer vision models (YOLOv8n, 
SAMURAI, YOLACT++) for construction safety applications 
•        Novel integration of AI detection outputs with GIS-based spatial risk mapping for 
multi-storey sites 
•        Development of occlusion-handling mechanisms tailored for crowded construction 
environments 
•        Production-ready safety monitoring system deployable in real-world construction 
projects

2.3 Frame Extraction from CCTV Footage 
CCTV video streams are converted to individual image frames for model training and testing. 
Extraction Parameters: 
•        Extraction Rate: 2 frames per second (2 FPS) from native 25 FPS video 
•        Sampling Strategy: Uniform temporal sampling (extract every 12.5th frame: 
frame indices 0, 12, 25, 37, ...) 
•        Output Format: JPEG images at 95% quality (minimal quality loss, ~60% file 
size reduction vs. PNG) 
•        Resolution: Maintain native 1920×1080 pixels (no downsampling during 
extraction) 
Rationale for 2 FPS Extraction Rate: 
•        Temporal Coverage: Workers on construction sites move at approximately 1 meter 
per second. At 2 FPS extraction, we capture worker positions every 0.5 seconds, 
providing adequate temporal resolution for safety monitoring. 
•        Redundancy Reduction: Adjacent frames at 25 FPS (40ms apart) are highly similar, 
resulting in training data redundancy. Sampling at 2 FPS (500ms apart) significantly 
reduces redundancy while preserving meaningful scene changes. 
•        Storage Efficiency: 2 FPS extraction reduces dataset size by 12.5× compared to 
using all frames (25 FPS), making data management feasible on standard hardware. 
•        Processing Speed: Smaller dataset enables faster preprocessing, annotation, and 
training iterations. 
Expected Frame Counts: 
•        Total CCTV footage: 300 hours × 3,600 seconds/hour × 2 FPS = 2,160,000 frames 
•        After motion detection filtering (only active construction periods): ~1,800,000 frames 
•        After duplicate removal (see Section 5.5): ~1,530,000 frames (15% reduction) 
 
 
 
2.4 Data Annotation 
Accurate annotations are critical for supervised model training. A semi-automated annotation 
workflow combines pre-trained model predictions with manual verification. 
Annotation Workflow: 
1. Pre-Annotation: Run pre-trained YOLOv8n (COCO weights) on all extracted 
frames to generate initial bounding box proposals. COCO model detects 'person' 
class which aligns with 'worker' class needed for construction safety. 
2.  Manual Refinement: Human annotators use Label Studio annotation tool to: 
verify and adjust bounding boxes proposed by pre-trained model, classify each 
detection into classes (Worker with Helmet, Worker without Helmet, Safety Vest, 
No Safety Vest), add missing detections (false negatives from pre-annotation), 
remove incorrect detections (false positives). 
3.  Quality Control: 10% of annotated frames randomly selected for secondary 
review by different annotator. Calculate inter-annotator agreement using 
Intersection over Union (IoU): if IoU >0.85 for >90% of bounding boxes, 
annotation quality is acceptable. Frames with disagreement resolved through 
consensus discussion. 
4.  Annotation Classes: Worker-Helmet (person wearing hard hat/helmet), Worker-
NoHelmet (person without helmet), SafetyVest (person wearing high-visibility 
vest), NoSafetyVest (person without vest), Heavy Equipment (excavators, 
concrete mixers, cranes - for hazard proximity analysis).

2.5 Preprocessing Pipeline 
Raw extracted frames undergo preprocessing to enhance quality and improve model training 
effectiveness. 
Preprocessing Steps: 
● Duplicate Removal: Calculate perceptual hash (pHash) for each frame 
using average hash algorithm (8×8 grayscale thumbnail). Compare hashes 
between consecutive frames: if Hamming distance <5 bits (out of 64), 
frames are considered duplicates. Remove duplicates, keeping only first 
occurrence. Expected reduction: 5-15% of frames (captures when camera 
views static scene with no worker movement). 
● Resolution Standardization: All frames resized to 1280×720 pixels (720p 
HD). Maintains aspect ratio by letterboxing (adding black bars if 
necessary). Rationale: standardized resolution ensures consistent input 
size for AI models and reduces storage requirements (from 1920×1080 to 
1280×720 = 55% pixel reduction). 
●  Brightness Normalization: Calculate average pixel intensity for each frame. 
If intensity <70 (scale 0-255), frame is classified as 'low-light' and 
undergoes gamma correction. Gamma correction formula: Output = 255 × 
(Input/255)^γ, where γ=0.7 for low-light enhancement. Post-correction, 
apply adaptive histogram equalization (CLAHE) to improve contrast in 
shadowed regions. 
●  Haze Detection & Enhancement: Calculate haze score using dark channel 
prior method: Dark Channel = min over RGB channels (min over 15×15 
pixel window (pixel intensity)). Haze score = 1 - (mean dark channel value / 
255). If haze score >0.4, frame is classified as 'dusty/hazy' and undergoes 
dehazing. Dehazing algorithm: Dark channel prior + guided filter for edge 
preservation. Dehazing parameters: ω=0.95 (atmosphere retention), t₀=0.1 
(minimum transmission). 
● Noise Reduction: Apply Gaussian blur with kernel size 3×3 and σ=0.5 to all 
frames. Reduces sensor noise and JPEG compression artifacts without 
significantly degrading edge sharpness. Particularly effective for night-time 
CCTV footage with higher noise levels.

3.3 Evaluation Metrics 
Model performance is quantified using standard object detection metrics, with emphasis on 
safety-critical measures (recall). 
Primary Metrics: 
•        Mean Average Precision (mAP): 
mAP@0.5: Average precision calculated at IoU (Intersection over Union) threshold of 0.5. A 
detection is considered correct if its IoU with ground truth ≥0.5. This is the standard PASCAL 
VOC metric, providing a lenient evaluation suitable for real-time applications where approximate 
localization is acceptable. 
mAP@0.5:0.95: Average precision calculated across IoU thresholds from 0.5 to 0.95 in 0.05 
increments (10 thresholds total), then averaged. This COCO metric provides stricter evaluation, 
rewarding precise bounding box localization. 
•        Precision: 
Precision = True Positives / (True Positives + False Positives). Measures the proportion of 
correct detections among all detections. High precision means low false alarm rate, reducing 
unnecessary safety alerts. 
•        Recall: 
Recall = True Positives / (True Positives + False Negatives). Measures the proportion of ground 
truth objects successfully detected. High recall means low missed detection rate, critical for 
safety (missing a worker in a hazard zone could be fatal). 
•        F1-Score: 
F1-Score = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall). Harmonic mean of precision and 
recall, providing balanced performance measure. Useful for comparing models with different 
precision-recall trade-offs. 
•        Inference Speed: 
Measured as Frames Per Second (FPS) or milliseconds per frame. Critical for real-time 
applications. Minimum requirement: 10 FPS for continuous monitoring (100ms latency per 
frame). 
 
 
 
Performance Targets:

3.4 Scenario-Based Evaluation 
Models are evaluated separately under four realistic construction site scenarios to assess 
robustness to environmental challenges. Each scenario tests 500 manually verified frames from 
the test set. 
3.4.1 Scenario 1: Normal Conditions 
•        Objective: Establish baseline performance under ideal conditions 
•        Conditions: Clear weather, good lighting (9 AM - 5 PM), moderate visibility 
(>90%), low-to-moderate worker density (1-3 workers per frame) 
•        Evaluation Weight: 20% (baseline reference point) 
•        Expected Performance: mAP@0.5 >0.85, Recall >0.90 (near-perfect detection in 
ideal conditions) 
 
 
3.4.2 Scenario 2: Dusty Environment 
•        Objective: Test robustness when dust/haze obscures visibility 
•        Conditions: Dusty or hazy weather, reduced visibility (50-80%), activities: 
excavation, demolition, material handling, haze score >0.4 (dark channel prior 
metric) 
•        Evaluation Weight: 25% (common condition during earth-moving activities) 
•        Expected Challenge: 10-15% reduction in recall due to obscured worker 
silhouettes 
•        Mitigation: Preprocessing with dehazing algorithm (Section 5.5.4) should 
recover ~5% of lost performance 
3.4.3 Scenario 3: Low-Light Conditions 
•        Objective: Ensure workers are detected in poor lighting (dawn/dusk) 
•        Conditions: Dawn (7:00-8:00 AM) or dusk (6:00-7:00 PM), low brightness (<80 
pixel intensity on 0-255 scale), heavy shadows from building structure 
•        Evaluation Weight: 25% (work often begins at dawn and continues to dusk) 
•        Expected Challenge: 15-20% reduction in recall, particularly for workers in 
heavily shadowed regions 
•        Mitigation: Gamma correction + adaptive histogram equalization (Section 
5.5.3) to brighten dark regions 
3.4.4 Scenario 4: Crowded Conditions 
•        Objective: Test ability to detect ALL workers in crowds - safety critical 
•        Conditions: ≥5 workers visible per frame, high worker density (>0.5 workers per 
100m² visible area), multiple workers in close proximity with occlusions, activities: 
team coordination, concrete pour, steel erection 
•        Evaluation Weight: 30% (highest weight due to safety criticality) 
•        Expected Challenge: 20-30% reduction in recall due to partial occlusions, 
overlapping bounding boxes, small object sizes (distant workers) 
•        Safety Importance: Missing even 1 worker in a crowded zone could result in 
injury/fatality. High recall is paramount. 
3.5 Model Selection Methodology 
The final model for deployment is selected through a weighted scoring approach that prioritizes 
safety-critical scenarios. 
Selection Formula: 
Total_Score = 0.20×(S1_mAP@0.5) + 0.25×(S2_mAP@0.5) + 0.25×(S3_mAP@0.5) + 
0.30×(S4_mAP@0.5) 
Decision Criteria: 
● Primary: Model with highest Total_Score is selected 
● Speed Constraint: Models achieving <10 FPS on target hardware (RTX 4050) 
are disqualified, regardless of accuracy score 
● Tie-Breaker: If two models have Total_Score difference <0.02 (within 2% of each 
other), select the model with higher recall in S4 Crowded scenario (safety 
priority) 
● Memory Constraint: If GPU memory usage during inference exceeds 5GB (out of 
6GB available), model is disqualified or requires quantization

4.2 Coordinate System Definition 
A local site coordinate system is established to facilitate accurate spatial referencing and ensure 
consistency across all GIS operations. 
•        Coordinate System Type: Local Cartesian coordinate system (metric units: 
meters) 
•        Origin Point (0,0): Southwest corner of construction site boundary 
•        X-Axis Direction: East (positive X increases eastward) 
•        Y-Axis Direction: North (positive Y increases northward) 
•        Z-Axis Direction: Vertical (positive Z increases upward, Z=0 at ground level) 
•        Units: Meters for all spatial measurements (X, Y, Z coordinates) 
For integration with global coordinate systems (e.g., GPS, satellite imagery): 
•        Global Reference System: WGS 84 / UTM Zone 44N (EPSG:32644) - appropriate 
for Tamil Nadu, India 
•        Site Origin (Global): Determined via DGPS survey, recorded as latitude/longitude 
(e.g., 10.7905°N, 79.1355°E) 
•        Transformation: Local coordinates converted to UTM using origin offset: UTM_X = 
UTM_Origin_X + Local_X, UTM_Y = UTM_Origin_Y + Local_Y 
 
4.3 Camera Calibration & Coordinate Transformation 
Camera calibration establishes the mathematical relationship between 2D image coordinates 
(pixels) and 3D world coordinates (meters), enabling accurate mapping of detected workers to 
GIS positions. 
4.3.1 Intrinsic Calibration 
Intrinsic parameters describe the camera's internal optical characteristics. 
•        Calibration Method: Zhang's method using checkerboard calibration pattern 
•        Calibration Pattern: 9×6 checkerboard with 50mm square size 
•        Calibration Images: 20 images of checkerboard at various orientations and 
distances (2-10 meters) 
•        Software: OpenCV calibrateCamera() function in Python 
Intrinsic Parameters Extracted: 
•        Focal Length (fx, fy): Millimeters, converted to pixels. Typical values for construction 
CCTV: fx ≈ 1200 pixels, fy ≈ 1200 pixels (assuming 1920×1080 sensor) 
•        Principal Point (cx, cy): Image center coordinates in pixels. Typically near image 
center: cx ≈ 960 pixels, cy ≈ 540 pixels 
•        Distortion Coefficients (k1, k2, p1, p2, k3): Radial (k) and tangential (p) lens 
distortion. Used to correct fish-eye effects from wide-angle lenses 
Camera Matrix (Intrinsic Matrix): 
K = [[fx, 0, cx], [0, fy, cy], [0, 0, 1]] 
4.3.2 Extrinsic Calibration 
Extrinsic parameters describe the camera's position and orientation in the 3D world coordinate 
system. 
•        Calibration Method: Measure 6+ ground control points (GCPs) with known 
world coordinates (X, Y, Z) and identify their pixel positions (u, v) in camera 
image 
•        GCP Selection: Distinctive site features visible in camera view: building 
corners, floor slab edges, equipment bases, survey markers 
•        GCP Measurement: Total station survey or high-precision DGPS (<5cm 
accuracy) 
•        Solving for Extrinsics: Perspective-n-Point (PnP) algorithm using OpenCV 
solvePnP() function 
Extrinsic Parameters Extracted: 
•        Rotation Matrix (R): 3×3 matrix describing camera orientation relative to world 
coordinate system. Derived from Rodrigues rotation vector (rotation axis and angle) 
•        Translation Vector (t): 3×1 vector [tx, ty, tz] describing camera position in world 
coordinates. Example: camera at height 4m looking down at 30° angle: t = [12, 15, 4] 
meters (X, Y, Z) 
 
4.3.3 Pixel-to-World Coordinate Transformation 
Transform detected worker bounding box coordinates from 2D image space to 3D world 
coordinates. 
Transformation Steps: 
● Extract Detection Coordinates: For each detected worker bounding box, 
compute bottom-center point (representing foot position): u = (bbox_x_min 
+ bbox_x_max) / 2, v = bbox_y_max (bottom of bounding box) 
● Undistort Coordinates: Apply inverse distortion correction using OpenCV 
undistortPoints() to obtain normalized image coordinates (x_n, y_n) 
●  Back-Project to 3D Ray: Compute 3D ray direction vector from camera 
center through image point: Ray_direction = R^(-1) × [[x_n], [y_n], [1]] 
●  Intersect with Ground Plane: Assuming worker is standing on a known 
floor level (e.g., Z=0 for ground floor, Z=3.5 for first floor): Compute 
intersection of ray with horizontal plane Z=floor_height. Solve: World_X = 
Camera_X + λ × Ray_X, World_Y = Camera_Y + λ × Ray_Y, floor_height = 
Camera_Z + λ × Ray_Z, where λ is ray parameter (distance along ray). Solve 
for λ, then compute (World_X, World_Y, floor_height) 
Floor Level Assignment: 
•        Method 1 - Y-Coordinate Lookup Table: For each camera, pre-compute vertical 
calibration lookup table mapping pixel row (v coordinate) to floor level. Example: v < 
300 pixels → Floor 1 (Z=3.5m), 300 ≤ v < 600 → Ground Floor (Z=0m), v ≥ 600 → 
Below ground level (exclude) 
•        Method 2 - Geometric Calculation: Use camera viewing geometry to infer floor level 
from worker position in image. Workers higher in frame (lower v pixel coordinate) are 
on higher floors. Geometric formula: Floor_Z = Camera_Z - (v - camera_tilt) × 
vertical_scale_factor

4.5 Heatmap Generation Algorithms 
4.5.1 Worker Density Heatmap 
Density heatmaps visualize worker concentration, identifying potential overcrowding and high-
traffic areas. 
Algorithm Steps: 
●  Grid Discretization: Overlay construction site with uniform 2×2 meter grid cells. 
Grid bounds: X from 0 to site_width, Y from 0 to site_length. Total grid cells: 
(site_width / 2) × (site_length / 2). Example: 40m × 60m site → 20 × 30 = 600 grid 
cells 
●  Temporal Window Selection: Define sliding time window for temporal 
aggregation. Default window: 5 minutes (balances real-time responsiveness with 
statistical stability). Query database for all detections within: current_time - 5 
minutes ≤ timestamp ≤ current_time 
● Density Calculation: For each grid cell, count number of worker detections with 
coordinates falling inside cell boundaries: Density = (Detection count in cell 
during time window) / (Cell area in m² × Time window in minutes). Units: workers 
per m² per minute. Example: 4 detections in 4 m² cell over 5 min → Density = 4 / 
(4 × 5) = 0.2 workers/m²/min 
● Kernel Density Estimation (KDE): Apply Gaussian kernel smoothing to 
produce continuous heatmap surface from discrete grid cells. Kernel 
function: G(d) = exp(-d² / (2σ²)), where d is distance from cell center, σ is 
bandwidth (standard deviation). Bandwidth selection: σ = 5 meters 
(approximately 2× grid cell size) for smooth interpolation. For each output 
pixel, compute weighted sum of nearby grid cell densities using Gaussian 
kernel 
● Color Mapping: Map smoothed density values to color gradient for visualization. 
Color scale: 0-0.1 workers/m²/min → Green (RGB: 0, 255, 0), 0.1-0.3 → Yellow 
(RGB: 255, 255, 0), 0.3-0.5 → Orange (RGB: 255, 165, 0), >0.5 → Red (RGB: 
255, 0, 0). Thresholds based on OSHA workspace density guidelines (safe 
capacity: <0.5 workers/m²) 
 
 
4.5.2 Risk-Based Heatmap 
Risk heatmaps integrate multiple safety factors to provide comprehensive spatial risk 
assessment beyond worker density alone. 
Multi-Factor Risk Model: 
Risk_Score = 0.35×Density_Factor + 0.30×PPE_Factor + 0.20×Hazard_Proximity_Factor + 
0.15×Movement_Factor 
Risk Factor Definitions: 
•        Density_Factor (0-1 scale): Normalized worker density relative to zone capacity. 
Calculation: Density_Factor = min(Current_Density / Max_Safe_Density, 1.0). 
Max_Safe_Density varies by work zone type: General circulation: 0.5 workers/m², 
Formwork area: 0.3 workers/m², Confined space: 0.2 workers/m². Example: Current 
density 0.4, Max safe 0.5 → Density_Factor = 0.4/0.5 = 0.8 
•        PPE_Factor (0-1 scale): Proportion of workers without required PPE. Calculation: 
PPE_Factor = (Workers_without_PPE) / (Total_workers). PPE requirements defined 
per zone (e.g., excavation requires helmet + vest + boots). Higher factor indicates 
elevated risk due to non-compliance. Example: 3 workers without helmets out of 10 
total → PPE_Factor = 0.3 
•        Hazard_Proximity_Factor (0-1 scale): Risk due to proximity to predefined hazard 
zones. Hazard types: open edges (fall risk), heavy equipment (collision risk), 
excavations (cave-in risk), electrical panels (electrocution risk). Calculation using 
Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW): For each worker, compute distance d to nearest 
hazard. Proximity_Score = 1 / (1 + d/5) if d ≤ 5 meters, else 0. (5-meter influence 
radius). Average proximity scores across all workers in grid cell. Example: Worker 
2m from open edge → Score = 1/(1+2/5) = 0.71 (high risk) 
•        Movement_Factor (0-1 scale): Indicates high-activity areas where accidents are 
more likely. Calculation: Compute standard deviation of worker positions over time 
window. High standard deviation → frequent worker movement → elevated risk. 
Normalization: Movement_Factor = min(Position_StdDev / 2.0, 1.0). (2 meters = 
threshold for 'high movement'). Example: Workers moving ~3m within 5min → 
StdDev ≈ 1.5m → Factor = 0.75

5.2 Phase B: GIS Justification & Spatial Readiness 
5.2.1 Conceptual GIS Framework 
The GIS integration conceptual framework establishes the theoretical foundation for 
spatial safety analysis in multi-storey construction environments. This framework 
addresses three core spatial challenges: 
● Spatial Resolution: How to accurately map 2D camera detections to 3D 
construction site coordinates across multiple floors 
● Temporal Dynamics: Methods for tracking worker movement patterns and 
occupancy density over time 
● Risk Aggregation: Approaches for combining detection data from multiple 
camera viewpoints into unified spatial risk metrics 
The framework proposes a layered GIS data model: 
• Base Layer: Site boundary, structural layout, floor plans (imported as CAD 
drawings or georeferenced images) 
• Zone Layer: Defined work zones (excavation, formwork, concrete pour, steel 
erection areas) with associated safety protocols 
• Detection Layer: Real-time worker positions derived from camera detections, 
updated at 2-second intervals 
• Risk Layer: Calculated risk scores based on worker density, PPE compliance 
rates, and proximity to hazard zones 
5.2.2 Site Layout Spatial Modeling

vector formats (shapefiles, GeoJSON) 
● Work Zone Definition: Delineation of functional areas based on construction 
schedule and safety protocols 
● Camera Coverage Mapping: Documentation of CCTV camera positions, viewing 
angles, and coverage overlap 
● Coordinate System Establishment: Definition of local site coordinate system 
aligned with true north for consistent spatial referencing

5.2.3 GIS Integration Justification 
The justification for GIS integration in multi-storey construction safety monitoring is 
established through three analytical perspectives: 
• Spatial Context Enhancement: Traditional computer vision provides object 
detection without spatial awareness. GIS integration enables: (a) identification of 
unauthorized workers in restricted zones, (b) proximity analysis to hazards (open 
edges, heavy equipment, material stockpiles), (c) emergency evacuation path 
optimization based on current worker locations 
 
• Multi-Floor Disambiguation: In multi-storey sites, single-camera views often 
capture workers on multiple floors. GIS integration resolves: (a) floor-level 
assignment of detected workers based on camera viewing geometry, (b) 
separate density calculations for each floor to prevent overcrowding, (c) floor-
specific risk assessments aligned with construction activities 
 
• Temporal-Spatial Analytics: GIS enables advanced spatial analytics not 
possible with vision systems alone: (a) worker movement pattern analysis to 
identify unsafe behaviors, (b) time-based occupancy heatmaps revealing peak 
congestion periods, (c) spatial clustering to detect unplanned worker gatherings 
that may indicate hazards 
This justification is supported by literature review of GIS applications in construction 
management and comparative analysis with conventional 2D monitoring approaches, 
demonstrating measurable improvements in spatial safety awareness.

6. Second Review Methodology 
The second review phase advances the project through comparative model evaluation 
and substantial GIS integration. This phase focuses on identifying the optimal computer 
vision architecture and developing functional spatial risk mapping capabilities. 
6.1 Phase A: Comparative Study & Occlusion Enhancement 
6.1.1 Multi-Model Training & Evaluation 
Three state-of-the-art computer vision architectures are trained and systematically 
compared to identify the model best suited for construction safety monitoring: 
• YOLOv8n (You Only Look Once v8 nano): Object detection network optimized 
for real-time inference. Architecture: Nano variant with 3.2M parameters, 
CSPDarknet backbone, PANet neck, detection head with anchor-free approach. 
Training on construction dataset using identical hyperparameters as First Review 
phase. 
 
• SAMURAI (Segment Anything Model for Unified Real-time Action and 
Instance segmentation): State-of-the-art video segmentation model. 
Architecture: Transformer-based with temporal attention mechanisms, memory-
efficient design for video processing. Expected advantage: Improved 
segmentation of partially occluded workers through temporal context. Training 
procedure: Fine-tuning SAM-2 base model on construction video sequences with 
5-frame temporal windows. 
 
 
• YOLACT++ (You Only Look At CoefficienTs++): Real-time instance 
segmentation network. Architecture: ResNet-101 backbone with Feature Pyramid 
Network (FPN), parallel prediction branches for masks and detection. Expected 
advantage: Pixel-level segmentation enabling precise boundary detection in 
crowded scenes. Training approach: End-to-end training on construction images 
with instance mask annotations. 
 
 
6.1.2 Comparative Evaluation Framework 
The three models are evaluated across multiple dimensions relevant to construction 
safety applications: 
1. Detection Accuracy Metrics 
• Mean Average Precision (mAP): mAP@0.5 (IoU threshold 0.5) and 
mAP@0.5:0.95 (IoU range from 0.5 to 0.95) 
• Precision: Ratio of correct detections to all detections (minimize false alarms) 
• Recall: Ratio of detected workers to all actual workers (minimize missed 
detections - safety critical) 
• F1-Score: Harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing balanced 
performance measure 
2. Inference Performance Metrics 
• Frames Per Second (FPS): Processing speed on target hardware (RTX 4050), 
minimum threshold of 10 FPS required for real-time monitoring 
• GPU Memory Utilization: Peak VRAM consumption during inference, critical for 
multi-camera deployment 
• Latency: End-to-end detection latency from frame capture to result output

4. Occlusion Handling Capability 
Special focus is placed on evaluating each model's ability to detect partially occluded

• Mild Occlusion: 25-50% of worker body obscured, head or torso visible

Detection rates are calculated separately for each occlusion category to quantify model 
robustness. Manual annotation of 200 frames with occlusion severity labels provides 
ground truth for this analysis. 
 
3.1.3 Model Selection & Occlusion Mechanism Integration 
The best-performing model is selected using a weighted scoring approach that 
prioritizes safety-critical scenarios: 
• Total Score = 0.20×(S1_mAP) + 0.25×(S2_mAP) + 0.25×(S3_mAP) + 
0.30×(S4_mAP) 
• Minimum FPS requirement: Models achieving <10 FPS are disqualified 
regardless of accuracy 
• Recall priority: In tie scenarios, higher recall model is selected to minimize 
missed detections 
Following model selection, occlusion-handling mechanisms are integrated to address 
identified limitations. Three enhancement strategies are explored: 
● Temporal Consistency Tracking: Implement multi-frame tracking using 
DeepSORT or ByteTrack algorithms. When a worker becomes occluded, their 
trajectory is predicted based on previous frames, maintaining detection continuity 
during temporary occlusions up to 2 seconds. 
● Multi-Camera Fusion: For regions covered by multiple cameras, combine 
detections from different viewpoints to resolve occlusions. A worker occluded in 
one view may be fully visible in another. Camera fusion algorithm: weighted 
averaging of detection confidence scores from overlapping coverage areas. 
● Occlusion-Aware Training: Augment training dataset with synthetically 
occluded images (randomly overlaying construction elements: beams, columns, 
equipment) to improve model robustness. Training augmentation: 30% of training 
images include synthetic occlusions with varying severity levels. 
The most effective enhancement strategy (or combination) is determined through 
ablation studies comparing detection performance before and after each intervention on 
the 500-frame crowded scenario test set. 
 
 
6.2 Phase B: GIS-Based Heatmap Generation (75% Completion) 
6.2.1 AI-GIS Integration Architecture 
The integration of AI detection outputs with GIS spatial analysis follows a systematic 
data pipeline: 
● Detection Data Collection: AI model generates detection results (bounding box 
coordinates, class labels, confidence scores) for each processed frame 
● Camera-to-World Coordinate Transformation: Convert 2D image coordinates 
(pixels) to 3D world coordinates (meters) using camera calibration parameters: 
intrinsic matrix (focal length, principal point), extrinsic matrix (rotation, translation 
relative to site origin), lens distortion coefficients 
● Floor-Level Assignment: Determine which floor each detected worker occupies 
using: vertical position in image frame, camera viewing angle geometry, 
comparison with known floor elevations from site model 
● Spatial Database Population: Store transformed coordinates in PostGIS 
(PostgreSQL with spatial extensions) database with schema: worker_detections 
(detection_id, timestamp, x_coord, y_coord, floor_level, class_label, confidence, 
camera_id) 
● Real-Time GIS Update: Stream detection data to QGIS via PostgreSQL 
connection, update point layers every 2 seconds with latest worker positions 
6.2.2 Worker Density Heatmap Generation 
Density heatmaps visualize worker concentration across the construction site, 
identifying areas of high occupancy that may pose safety risks. The heatmap generation 
methodology consists of: 
• Spatial Discretization: Divide construction site into 2×2 meter grid cells, 
each cell representing a distinct spatial analysis unit 
• Temporal Aggregation: Aggregate worker positions over sliding 5-minute 
time windows to capture short-term occupancy patterns while filtering 
transient detections 
• Density Calculation: For each grid cell, compute worker density as: Density 
= (Number of detections in cell during time window) / (Cell area in m² × 
Time window in minutes). Units: workers per m² per minute 
• Kernel Density Estimation: Apply Gaussian kernel smoothing to raw 
density values to produce continuous heatmap surface. Bandwidth 
parameter: 5 meters (approximately 2× grid cell size) for smooth 
interpolation between cells 
• Color Mapping: Map density values to color gradient: Green (0-0.1 
workers/m²/min) → Yellow (0.1-0.3) → Orange (0.3-0.5) → Red (>0.5 
workers/m²/min). Thresholds based on OSHA workspace density 
recommendations 
 
6.2.3 Risk-Based Heatmap Generation 
Risk heatmaps integrate multiple safety factors beyond worker density to provide 
comprehensive spatial risk assessment. Risk calculation methodology: 
• Multi-Factor Risk Model: Combine four risk components with weighted 
aggregation: Risk_Score = 0.35×Density_Factor + 0.30×PPE_Factor + 
0.20×Hazard_Proximity_Factor + 0.15×Movement_Factor 
Component Definitions: 
• Density_Factor: Normalized worker density (0-1 scale) relative to maximum safe 
capacity for work zone type 
• PPE_Factor: Proportion of detected workers without required PPE (helmets, 
safety vests). Higher values indicate elevated risk 
• Hazard_Proximity_Factor: Distance-weighted score based on proximity to 
predefined hazard zones (open edges, heavy equipment, excavations). Inverse 
distance weighting with 5-meter influence radius 
• Movement_Factor: Standard deviation of worker positions over time window, 
indicating high-activity areas where accidents are more likely

7.1 Phase A: Real-Time Occlusion-Aware AI Deployment 
7.1.1 Production Environment Configuration 
The selected AI model (with occlusion enhancements from Second Review) is 
optimized for production deployment on construction site hardware: 
• Model Optimization: Conversion to ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange) 
format for cross-platform compatibility. Quantization to INT8 precision (8-
bit integers) to reduce model size by ~75% and increase inference speed by 
~2-3×, with minimal accuracy loss (<2% mAP degradation). TensorRT 
optimization for NVIDIA GPUs, achieving maximum throughput on RTX 
hardware. 
• Multi-Camera Processing: Parallel inference pipeline for simultaneous 
processing of 4-6 CCTV camera feeds. Implementation using Python 
multiprocessing with dedicated GPU stream per camera. Frame buffering to 
prevent dropped frames during processing spikes. 
• Hardware Configuration: Deployment server specifications: Intel i7 12th 
gen CPU (16 threads), NVIDIA RTX 4050 GPU (6GB VRAM), 32GB DDR5 
RAM, 512GB NVMe SSD. Network: Gigabit Ethernet for RTSP camera 
streams, low-latency network switch (<5ms latency). 
7.1.2 Crowded Environment Optimization 
Specific optimizations target performance in crowded multi-storey construction 
scenarios (>5 workers per frame), the most challenging condition identified in first two 
reviews: 
1. Adaptive NMS Thresholding: Standard Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) 
is adjusted dynamically based on detected worker count. Low density (<3 
workers): NMS threshold = 0.45 (standard). Medium density (3-7 workers): 
NMS threshold = 0.35 (more permissive, retain overlapping detections). 
High density (>7 workers): NMS threshold = 0.25 (most permissive, critical 
to avoid missed detections). 
2. Crowd Density Pre-Filtering: Before processing each frame, compute 
crowd density estimate using fast background subtraction. If density 
exceeds threshold, trigger specialized processing: reduced inference 
resolution for speed (480×480 instead of 640×640), increased confidence 
threshold to 0.6 (filter low-confidence false positives common in crowds), 
enable temporal tracking to link detections across frames. 
3. Multi-Scale Detection: Implement image pyramid processing for frames 
with high worker density. Original image processed at 640×640 (standard), 
downsampled image at 480×480 (captures distant/small workers), 
upsampled crops at 800×800 for dense regions (improves small object 
detection). Detections from all scales merged using Weighted Boxes 
Fusion (WBF) algorithm. 
7.1.3 Multi-Storey Floor Disambiguation 
For multi-storey construction sites where single camera views capture multiple floors, 
accurate floor assignment is critical for correct spatial risk mapping. Floor 
disambiguation methodology: 
• Vertical Axis Calibration: For each camera, establish vertical calibration by 
identifying known reference points on each floor (e.g., floor slabs, window 
ledges). Create vertical coordinate lookup table mapping pixel row (Y-
coordinate) to floor level. 
• Bounding Box Centroid Analysis: Extract Y-coordinate of worker bounding 
box centroid (bottom center point, as it represents foot position closest to 
floor). Match centroid Y-coordinate to calibration table to determine floor 
level with confidence score. 
• Multi-Frame Validation: Track worker across 5 consecutive frames (2-
second window at 2 FPS processing rate). If floor assignment fluctuates 
(worker appears to jump between floors), apply majority voting to assign 
most frequent floor level. Flag detections with inconsistent floor 
assignments for manual review. 
• Confidence Thresholding: Only assign floor level if confidence >0.7. 
Detections with confidence <0.7 are marked as 'ambiguous floor' and 
excluded from floor-specific GIS layers but included in overall site-wide 
heatmaps.

8. Conclusion 
This methodology document provides a comprehensive roadmap for the development and 
deployment of BuildSight, an integrated AI-powered construction safety monitoring system with 
GIS-based spatial risk analysis. The project addresses critical challenges in construction site 
safety through systematic application of computer vision technology and geographic information 
systems. 
8.1 Key Methodological Contributions 
The methodology presented in this document contributes to construction safety research 
through several novel approaches: 
•        Dual-Track Parallel Execution: Simultaneous advancement of AI model 
development (Phase A) and GIS spatial analysis (Phase B) across all three 
review cycles ensures cohesive integration and prevents sequential 
dependency bottlenecks. 
•        Scenario-Based Evaluation Framework: Systematic testing under four realistic 
construction conditions (normal, dusty, low-light, crowded) with weighted 
scoring prioritizing safety-critical scenarios provides robust model validation 
beyond standard benchmark metrics. 
•        Multi-Factor Spatial Risk Model: Integration of worker density, PPE 
compliance, hazard proximity, and movement patterns into unified risk 
heatmaps offers comprehensive spatial safety assessment beyond traditional 
density-only approaches. 
•        Multi-Storey Floor Disambiguation: Explicit methodology for assigning 
detected workers to correct floor levels in multi-storey sites addresses a 
significant gap in existing construction monitoring systems. 
•        Production-Ready System Design: Emphasis on real-time performance (≥10 FPS), 
system reliability (99% uptime), and practical deployment considerations ensures 
research outcomes translate to industry-deployable solutions.

8.3 Scalability & Future Extensions 
While this methodology focuses on a G+1 residential construction project, the system 
architecture is designed for scalability and extensibility: 
•        Camera Scaling: Multi-camera processing pipeline supports 4-12 
simultaneous camera streams with minimal performance degradation through 
GPU stream parallelization and efficient data pipelining. 
•        Site Scaling: GIS framework accommodates sites of varying complexity (from 
single-storey to high-rise buildings) through flexible coordinate system 
definition and hierarchical floor modeling. 
•        Detection Class Extension: Current methodology focuses on worker + PPE 
detection but can be extended to detect additional safety hazards: equipment 
violations (e.g., scaffolding without guardrails), unsafe behaviors (e.g., 
workers not using harnesses at height), material stockpile hazards (e.g., 
improperly secured loads). 
•        Advanced Analytics: Temporal-spatial analytics layer can be added for: 
Predictive accident risk modeling based on historical patterns, Worker 
productivity analysis from movement patterns, Construction progress 
monitoring aligned with BIM schedules.

The BuildSight methodology represents a rigorous, academically sound approach to developing

technology with geographic information systems, the project addresses fundamental limitations 
of conventional safety monitoring approaches that lack spatial awareness and struggle with 
occlusion challenges in complex multi-storey environments. 
The systematic three-phase execution strategy ensures incremental progress with clear 
milestones and success criteria at each stage. The emphasis on real-world validation, 
production-ready implementation, and industry deployment distinguishes this research from 
purely theoretical academic work, positioning BuildSight as a practical solution to pressing 
construction safety challenges.
